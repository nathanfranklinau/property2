If you have ever looked at your property title or council rates notice, you have probably seen something like **"Lot 5 on SP123456"**. This is your property's cadastral reference — a unique identifier in Queensland's land registry system.

Understanding what these numbers mean helps you make sense of your property boundaries, title history, and subdivision potential.

## What Is Cadastral Data?

The word "cadastral" comes from the French *cadastre*, meaning a register of property. In Australia, cadastral data is the official record of:

- **Property boundaries** — the exact shape and dimensions of each lot
- **Lot and plan identifiers** — the unique reference for every piece of land
- **Areas** — the surveyed area of each lot in square metres

This data is maintained by **Titles Queensland** (part of the Department of Resources) and is the legal source of truth for property boundaries.

## Understanding Lot and Plan Numbers

Every property in Queensland is identified by a **lot number** on a **registered plan**. The plan types tell you about the property's history:

### RP — Registered Plan

The oldest plan type. Properties on RP plans were typically surveyed during the original subdivision of an area — often decades ago. If your property is on an RP, it has likely been in its current configuration for a long time.

### SP — Survey Plan

A more modern plan type. SP plans are created when land is **subdivided** — so if your property is on an SP, it was created by a subdivision at some point. The SP number tells you roughly when the subdivision happened (higher numbers are more recent).

### BUP — Building Units Plan

Used for **unit titles** (strata). If your property is on a BUP, it is a unit or apartment within a larger building or complex.

### GTP — Group Titles Plan

Similar to BUP but for **group title** schemes — typically townhouse complexes where each owner has their own lot plus shared common property.

## What a Survey Plan Shows

A registered survey plan is a technical document prepared by a licensed surveyor. It includes:

- **Lot boundaries** with precise dimensions (bearings and distances)
- **Lot area** in square metres
- **Easements** — areas where utilities or drainage have legal access rights
- **Connection to the wider survey fabric** — how the lot relates to surrounding properties
- **Plan of subdivision** — showing original and new lot configurations (for SP plans)

## Why This Matters for Your Property

Understanding your cadastral data helps you:

1. **Confirm your actual lot size** — the surveyed area, not just what the real estate listing said
2. **Identify easements** — which parts of your land have restrictions
3. **Check subdivision history** — has your lot been subdivided before? Properties on SP plans already have
4. **Understand frontage and dimensions** — critical for assessing subdivision potential
5. **Verify boundaries** — important before building near boundaries or installing fences

## Where to Find Your Cadastral Information

- **Rates notice** — your lot and plan number is printed on every council rates notice
- **Property title** — the Certificate of Title shows lot/plan, area, and registered easements
- **QLD Globe** — the state government's free mapping tool shows cadastral boundaries
- **PropertyProfiler** — we display your lot, plan, and surveyed area when you analyse your property

## Common Misconceptions

**"My fence is on the boundary"** — fences are often not on the actual surveyed boundary. They may have been built based on an approximation, an old peg that has moved, or a neighbourly agreement. Only a licensed surveyor can confirm the true boundary position.

**"My lot size is what the real estate agent told me"** — always verify against the cadastral data. Real estate listings sometimes round numbers or use approximate measurements.

**"I can build right up to my boundary"** — even on your own land, building regulations require setbacks from boundaries. These vary by zone and building type.

## Key Takeaway

Your lot and plan number is the foundation of your property identity. Before making any decisions about your property, make sure you understand the basics — your actual lot size, plan type, and any easements that might affect what you can do.
