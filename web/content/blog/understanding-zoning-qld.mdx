Every property in Queensland sits within a **zone** defined by your local council's planning scheme. That zone determines what you can and cannot do with your land — from building a granny flat to subdividing into multiple lots.

Understanding your zone is one of the first things you should do before making any plans for your property.

## What Is Zoning?

Zoning is how local governments control land use. Each zone has rules about:

- **What you can build** (houses, units, shops, sheds)
- **How much you can build** (height limits, site cover percentages)
- **Minimum lot sizes** for subdivision
- **Setback requirements** (how far buildings must be from boundaries)
- **Density** (how many dwellings per hectare)

Your property's zone is set by your local council's **planning scheme** — a legally binding document that maps every piece of land in the council area to a specific zone.

## Common Residential Zones in QLD

While exact names vary between councils, most QLD planning schemes include zones similar to these:

### Low Density Residential

The most common zone for suburban houses. Typically allows:
- Detached houses
- Secondary dwellings (granny flats) in some cases
- Subdivision down to 400-600 m² lots (varies by council)
- Maximum 2 storeys

### Medium Density Residential

Allows more intensive development:
- Townhouses, duplexes, and small unit blocks
- Smaller minimum lot sizes (often 300-400 m²)
- Usually up to 3 storeys

### Character Residential

Found in older suburbs with heritage value:
- Stricter building controls to preserve neighbourhood character
- Often limits demolition of existing dwellings
- May have larger minimum lot sizes
- Subdivision can be more difficult

### Rural Residential

Large lot areas on urban fringes:
- Minimum lot sizes of 2,000 m² to 4,000 m² or more
- Limited subdivision potential
- Typically allows one dwelling per lot plus ancillary structures

### Emerging Community

Land earmarked for future urban development:
- Subdivision generally not permitted until a more detailed plan is prepared
- Can be rezoned over time as cities grow

## How to Find Your Property's Zone

There are several ways to check:

1. **Your council's online mapping tool** — most QLD councils have an interactive map showing zones for every property
2. **QLD Government PD Hub** — the state-wide planning portal at pdonline.dsdmip.qld.gov.au
3. **PropertyProfiler** — we look up zoning data automatically when you analyse your property

## How Zoning Affects Subdivision

The key factors that zoning controls for subdivision are:

- **Minimum lot size** — both the new lot and remaining lot must meet this
- **Minimum frontage** — each lot needs adequate road access
- **Assessment pathway** — code assessment (faster, cheaper) vs impact assessment (slower, requires public notification)
- **Overlays** — additional restrictions layered on top of the base zone

For example, in Brisbane City Council's Low Density Residential zone, the minimum lot size for subdivision is generally 400 m². But if your property also has a Character Residential overlay, the minimum may be larger and additional conditions apply.

## Overlays: The Hidden Layer

Overlays sit on top of zones and add extra requirements. Common ones include:

- **Flood overlay** — restricts building in flood-prone areas
- **Bushfire overlay** — requires specific construction standards
- **Heritage overlay** — protects buildings or sites of cultural significance
- **Character overlay** — preserves the look and feel of a neighbourhood
- **Infrastructure overlay** — land reserved for future roads, rail, or utilities

A property can have multiple overlays active at the same time. Always check overlays alongside your base zone.

## What If My Zone Does Not Permit What I Want?

Options include:

- **Applying for a code or impact assessable development** — some uses are not prohibited, just require assessment
- **Requesting a planning scheme amendment** — rare and slow, but possible for significant proposals
- **Waiting for rezoning** — councils periodically review and update their planning schemes

In most cases, it is best to work within your existing zone rather than trying to change it.

## Key Takeaway

Before making any decisions about your property — whether it is building an extension, adding a granny flat, or subdividing — check your zone first. It sets the boundaries for everything else.
