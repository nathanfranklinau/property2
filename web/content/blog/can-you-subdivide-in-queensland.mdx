Subdividing your land in Queensland is one of the most effective ways to unlock value from your property. Whether you want to build a second dwelling, sell off a vacant lot, or create space for a granny flat, understanding the process is the first step.

Here is a practical overview of what is involved.

## The 7 Stages of Subdivision in QLD

### 1. Eligibility Assessment

Before spending any money, confirm the basics:

- **Minimum lot size** — check your local council's planning scheme. Most residential zones require 400-600 m² per new lot. Both the new lot *and* the remaining lot must meet the minimum.
- **Zoning** — not all zones permit subdivision as of right. Some require impact assessment, which is slower and more expensive.
- **Frontage** — each new lot typically needs a minimum road frontage (6-10 m for battle-axe access, 15 m+ for standard lots).
- **Overlays and constraints** — flood, bushfire, heritage, or character overlays may restrict what you can do. Check for easements on your title.

### 2. Pre-Lodgement

Once you have confirmed basic eligibility:

- **Engage a town planner** — not legally required, but strongly recommended. They will advise whether your application is code-assessable (faster) or impact-assessable.
- **Pre-lodgement meeting** — most councils offer this for $100-$500. It identifies likely conditions before you commit to full plans.
- **Engage a licensed surveyor** — they prepare the Survey Plan and identify boundary pegs and easements.

### 3. Development Application (DA)

Your town planner or you can prepare and lodge the DA via the QLD Government's PD Hub portal:

- A site plan showing proposed lot boundaries and dimensions
- A statement of proposal or town planning report
- Stormwater management and services plans (if required)
- Council application fees typically range from $500 to $5,000 for residential

### 4. Council Assessment

After lodgement, the statutory clock starts:

- **Code assessment**: 20 business days
- **Impact assessment**: longer, includes a public notification period

Council may issue an **Information Request** for additional details. The clock stops while you respond.

You will receive a **Decision Notice** — either approval with conditions, or refusal.

### 5. Post-Approval Works

If approved, you will likely need to:

- Pay **infrastructure charges** ($20,000-$60,000+ depending on council)
- Complete required works (kerb and channel, stormwater, driveway upgrades)
- Connect the new lot to **water, sewer, and electricity**

### 6. Survey Plan Sealing

Your surveyor prepares the final Survey Plan based on approved conditions. Council checks all conditions are met, then seals the plan.

### 7. Title Registration

The sealed Survey Plan is lodged with **Titles Queensland**. Once registered, each new lot receives its own Certificate of Title. The subdivision is complete.

## What Does It Cost?

| Item | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Town planner | $2,000 - $8,000 |
| Licensed surveyor | $3,000 - $8,000 |
| Council DA fee | $500 - $5,000 |
| Infrastructure charges | $20,000 - $60,000+ |
| Civil works | $10,000 - $50,000+ |
| Utility connections | $5,000 - $20,000 |
| Titles registration | ~$800 |
| **Total** | **$50,000 - $150,000+** |

Costs vary significantly depending on your council, the complexity of conditions, and the level of civil works required.

## How Long Does It Take?

Expect **6 to 18 months** from initial assessment to new titles. Straightforward code-assessable applications in responsive councils can be faster. Complex applications with impact assessment or significant infrastructure works take longer.
