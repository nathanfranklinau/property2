When Queensland's cadastral database records a piece of land, it classifies it into one of six **parcel types**. This classification tells you the fundamental nature of the parcel — whether it is a private lot, a road, a waterway, an easement corridor, or something else entirely.

Understanding parcel types helps you read a cadastral map, interpret a title search, and understand what your neighbours' parcels actually are.

## The Six Parcel Types in Queensland

Queensland's 3.5 million cadastral parcels break down as follows:

| Parcel Type | Count | Share |
|---|---|---|
| Lot Type Parcel | 2,550,004 | 73.6% |
| Road Type Parcel | 382,744 | 11.0% |
| Easement | 309,258 | 8.9% |
| Unlinked parcel or interest | 194,704 | 5.6% |
| Watercourse | 29,186 | 0.8% |
| Transport Route | 128 | &lt;0.1% |

## Lot Type Parcel — Private and Public Land

**2.55 million parcels — 74% of all QLD parcels**

A "Lot Type Parcel" is the standard land parcel — a defined area of land with a lot/plan number, owned by a person, company, or the Crown, and having a registered title.

This includes:
- **Suburban house blocks** — your standard residential allotment
- **Rural and agricultural land** — farms, stations, rural residential lots
- **Commercial and industrial lots** — factory sites, retail parcels, offices
- **Parks and reserves** — Crown land parcels dedicated to public use
- **National parks and state forests** — large Crown parcels under environmental legislation
- **Unit lots** — individual lots within a strata or community title scheme

The "Lot Type" label just means it is a standard bounded parcel with a registered title. It says nothing about what the land is used for or what tenure applies — those are recorded separately.

When you look up a property address, the result is almost always a Lot Type Parcel.

## Road Type Parcel — Road Reserves

**382,744 parcels — 11% of QLD cadastre**

Road Type Parcels define the legal extent of **road reserves** — the corridors of Crown land set aside for roads, footpaths, and associated infrastructure. Every street, highway, lane, and access track in Queensland has a corresponding Road Type Parcel in the cadastre.

Key things to know:

- Road reserves are wider than the sealed road surface. They include kerbs, footpaths, nature strips, drainage infrastructure, and sometimes underground utilities.
- The **road reserve boundary** — not the kerb — is the legal boundary of your property.
- Structures (fences, letterboxes, garden beds) placed in the road reserve technically sit on Crown land. Councils issue permits for this.
- **Secondary dwellings and outbuildings** cannot be built in the road reserve setback.
- When subdividing, each new lot must have **frontage to a road reserve** — not just a physical road surface.

The average road reserve in residential areas is 15–20 metres wide. State-controlled roads (highways, arterials) often have reserves of 40 metres or more.

## Easement — Legal Corridors Across Land

**309,258 parcels — 9% of QLD cadastre**

An Easement parcel in the cadastre represents a legally defined **strip of land across which someone has a registered right** — typically a utility company, a council, or an adjoining landowner.

The underlying land still belongs to the lot owner. The easement just restricts what can be done on that strip and grants the easement holder rights of access.

Common easement types recorded as parcels:
- **Drainage easements** — stormwater pipes, channels, and overland flow paths
- **Sewerage easements** — council sewer mains running through private land
- **Electricity easements** — Energex or Powerlink infrastructure corridors
- **Water supply easements** — Council or Seqwater pipelines
- **Access easements** — rights of way for a neighbour to cross your land to reach theirs

**Why this matters for development:**

Easements are one of the most commonly overlooked constraints. You cannot build a permanent structure over a registered easement without the easement holder's consent — and they will almost always say no.

Before designing a new dwelling, shed, pool, or extension, always check your title for registered easements and overlay their locations on your plan. PropertyProfiler flags easements in the analysis results.

## Unlinked Parcel or Interest

**194,704 parcels — 6% of QLD cadastre**

This is a catch-all category for parcels that are recorded in the cadastral database but **cannot be definitively linked to a current title** or that represent a registered interest without a fully defined boundary.

This category includes:
- **Historical parcels** that predate modern survey methods and have poorly defined boundaries
- **Pending parcels** created by a survey plan that has been lodged but not yet fully processed
- **Mining interests, licences, and permits** that overlay other parcels
- **Common property** in older community title schemes where the common area is not a discrete lot

In practice, "unlinked" parcels mostly appear in rural areas, remote regions, and older subdivisions. If a parcel near your property is classified as unlinked, it can indicate title uncertainty or an older layout that predates digital cadastral mapping.

## Watercourse — Rivers, Creeks, and Tidal Land

**29,186 parcels**

Watercourse parcels define the **bed and banks of rivers, creeks, lakes, and tidal lands** in Queensland. These areas belong to the Crown under the *Water Act 2000* and the *Land Act 1994*.

What this means practically:

- **You do not own the creek running through your property.** The watercourse parcel is Crown land.
- The boundary of your lot follows the **top of the bank** or the **mean high water mark**, not the water's edge.
- Building within or over a watercourse requires approvals from the Department of Resources and often also the Department of Environment.
- **Riparian rights** — the rights of a landholder adjacent to a watercourse — are defined by state legislation, not the cadastre.

Watercourse parcels appear as irregular linear features on cadastral maps, usually following natural channels. They are often accompanied by a drainage easement on adjacent private lots.

If your property boundary is listed as a **natural boundary** (as opposed to a surveyed line), it means it follows a watercourse — and that boundary can move over time as the watercourse shifts.

## Transport Route

**128 parcels**

Transport Route parcels define corridors reserved for **major transport infrastructure** — primarily railway lines, busway corridors, and future transport reservations.

These are distinct from Road Type Parcels (which cover all roads) and instead capture:
- Active QR and Aurizon rail lines
- Busway and transitway corridors (e.g. South East Busway)
- Future rail reservations identified in state infrastructure plans

Transport Route parcels are Crown land managed by the state. Like road reserves, they cannot be privately owned or developed, and they impose setback requirements on adjacent private lots.

## How to Read a Cadastral Map

When you look at a cadastral map of your suburb, you are looking at the full set of parcel types laid out together:

- The large polygons with lot/plan numbers are **Lot Type Parcels** — private and public land
- The linear parcels running between them are **Road Type Parcels** — road reserves
- The hatched strips crossing through lots are **Easements**
- The irregular sinuous shapes following drains and creeks are **Watercourses**

Understanding what each shape represents helps you read the map accurately — rather than assuming every polygon is something you can own.

## Why Parcel Type Matters for Development

**Frontage:** A lot must front a Road Type Parcel to be subdivided. Frontage onto an easement or watercourse does not count.

**Setbacks:** Building setbacks are measured from the road reserve boundary — the edge of the Road Type Parcel — not from the kerb.

**Easement constraints:** Before lodging a building application, identify all Easement parcels on or near your lot and confirm no proposed works cross them.

**Boundary type:** If your lot boundary follows a Watercourse parcel, the boundary is legal but not fixed — it can shift with the channel.

**Access:** Landlocked lots (no road frontage) typically rely on Access Easements across neighbouring Lot Type Parcels. These must be properly registered to be enforceable.
