When you look at a property title in Queensland, one of the first things recorded is the **tenure** — the legal basis on which someone holds that land. It determines what rights you have, what you can do with the land, and who ultimately owns it.

Most Australians only ever deal with one type of tenure. But Queensland has 22 distinct tenure categories across 3.5 million land parcels, reflecting everything from suburban backyards to national parks to underground mining rights.

## Freehold — The Most Common Tenure

**2.42 million parcels (70% of all QLD land parcels)**

Freehold is what most people think of as "owning" land. You hold the title in perpetuity, can sell it, mortgage it, build on it, and leave it to your heirs. The Crown (state government) has ceded permanent ownership to you.

Freehold comes in several forms:
- **Fee simple** — absolute ownership with no conditions
- **Strata title** — freehold ownership of a defined space within a building (units, apartments)
- **Community title** — freehold lots with shared common property

If you own a house in the suburbs, you almost certainly hold freehold title.

## Easement — Rights Across Someone Else's Land

**309,256 parcels**

An easement is not really an ownership type — it is a legal right to use part of another person's land for a specific purpose. Easements appear as separate entries in the cadastre because they have defined geographic boundaries.

Common easements in QLD:
- **Drainage easements** — underground pipes or channels crossing your block
- **Electricity easements** — power line corridors (Energex, Powerlink)
- **Access easements** — rights of way allowing a neighbour to cross your land
- **Sewerage easements** — council sewer infrastructure

Easements typically restrict what you can build on that strip of land. They show up on your title and on subdivision plans as a coloured hatched area.

## Lands Lease — Leasehold from the Crown

**47,293 parcels**

A Lands Lease (historically called a "pastoral lease") is a long-term lease of Crown land, primarily used for grazing and agriculture across regional and outback Queensland. The state government retains ownership; the lessee pays rent and has the right to use the land for specified purposes.

Key characteristics:
- Fixed term (commonly 30–100 years), renewable
- Conditions on land use — you cannot simply build a subdivision
- Can sometimes be converted to freehold, but this requires a formal application
- Common in western and remote QLD where large pastoral properties dominate

## Reserve — Land Set Aside for Public Purposes

**38,352 parcels**

Reserves are Crown land set aside for a specific community purpose. Local councils typically manage them as trustees on behalf of the state.

Common reserve purposes:
- **Recreation** — parks, sportsgrounds, open space
- **Environmental** — conservation areas, wetlands
- **Public utilities** — drainage, water supply, sewerage
- **Roads** — road reserve corridors
- **Cemeteries, showgrounds, boat ramps**

Reserves cannot be sold, subdivided, or developed for private use. They appear on property searches and can affect what you can do on adjacent lots (e.g. noise, setbacks, views).

## State Land — Unallocated Crown Land

**16,910 parcels**

Unallocated State Land (USL) is Crown land that has not been allocated to any particular tenure or purpose. It is not reserved, not leased, and not freehold — it simply belongs to the state and sits idle.

This land can be:
- Applied for as a lease or freehold purchase through the Department of Resources
- Allocated as a reserve for a new public purpose
- Traded between government departments

USL is common in remote areas, coastal margins, and around watercourses.

## Covenant — Restrictions on Use

**13,043 parcels**

A covenant is a legal agreement — often between a developer and a council or state government — that restricts how land can be used in perpetuity. Unlike easements, covenants don't grant anyone a right of access; they simply prevent certain things from happening.

Examples:
- **Conservation covenants** — no clearing of native vegetation
- **Drainage covenants** — no filling or obstructing a drainage path
- **Building covenants** — developer-imposed restrictions on building materials or style (common in new estates)

Covenants run with the land, meaning they bind future owners even if the original parties are long gone.

## National Park — Protected Natural Areas

**5,352 parcels**

National Parks are Crown land permanently protected under the *Nature Conservation Act 1992*. They cannot be sold, leased for private use, or developed. Management is by the Department of Environment.

Adjacent freehold land can be affected by proximity to national parks — bushfire management obligations, vegetation clearing restrictions, and biosecurity requirements are more stringent near protected areas.

## Below the Depth Plans — Volumetric Titles

**4,526 parcels**

This is a relatively modern tenure type covering **volumetric (three-dimensional) lots** — typically used in large mixed-use developments and infrastructure projects where ownership is defined in three dimensions, not just surface area.

Common uses:
- Underground car parks or infrastructure beneath a building
- Airspace lots above a road or rail corridor
- Basement and tunnel rights in CBD developments

The name comes from plans that define the parcel boundary as starting *below* a certain depth from the surface.

## State Forest and Timber/Forest Reserve

**2,463 + 49 parcels**

State Forests are Crown land managed by Forestry and Wood Products Queensland for timber production, recreation, and conservation. Timber Reserves are a related (and now largely historical) tenure used for softwood plantation areas.

Like national parks, these cannot be privately owned or developed. They appear on property searches and may impose vegetation management obligations on nearby freehold land.

## Profit à Prendre — Rights to Take Resources

**1,102 parcels**

A *profit à prendre* (French for "profit to take") is a right to enter land and take something from it — timber, soil, minerals, water, or crops. It is a very old common law concept that still appears in the QLD cadastre.

In practice today, these are mostly:
- Rights to graze stock on another's land
- Rights to extract sand, gravel, or other materials
- Historical timber rights

They are registered against the burdened title and show up in title searches.

## Mines Tenure

**268 parcels**

Mines Tenure covers land granted under Queensland's mining and resources legislation — primarily the *Mineral Resources Act 1989*. This includes mining leases, mineral development licences, and exploration permits.

Importantly, in Australia the Crown retains ownership of minerals beneath the surface even on freehold land. Mines Tenure records the surface rights granted to resource companies to access and extract those minerals.

## Why Tenure Matters for Property Decisions

**Buying:** Always confirm tenure before purchasing. A property described as "200 acres" might include a mix of freehold, lease, and reserve — with very different rights attached to each.

**Subdividing:** Only freehold land can be subdivided and sold as separate freehold titles. Leasehold land requires different processes and approvals.

**Building:** Development approvals assume freehold title. Reserves, leases, and state land require separate applications through the Department of Resources.

**Investing:** Leasehold properties can appear cheap per hectare but come with rent obligations, use restrictions, and no guarantee of renewal.

**Adjacent land:** Reserve and state land next door can be a positive (permanent open space, views preserved) or a constraint (limited road access, no future development buffer).
